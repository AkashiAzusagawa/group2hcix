ELEMENTS OF WINDOWING SYSTEMS

The capacity of a windowing system to allow programmer freedom from the particulars of the hardware devices is one of its primary selling points. A typical workstation will consist of a keyboard, some kind of visual display screen, and a pointing device, such as a mouse. Each of these hardware devices is unique in terms of the data that it communicates and the instructions that are used to direct it, but they may all be utilized in any interactive system. These hardware devices can come in a wide range of forms.

Any particular windowing system will have a predetermined generic language for the abstract terminal; this language will be referred to as its imaging model. The imaging models are enough to describe pictures with a high degree of freedom. For reasons related to performance, certain primitives are used to manage text pictures, either in the form of particular pixel images or as definitions of fonts with a more general scope.

EXAMPLE OF IMAGING MODELS

Pixels - The information that is shown on the screen is made up of a grid of columns and rows of dots, also known as pixels, each of which may be individually turned on or off and assigned a color. This is a standard imaging paradigm for personal computers, and the X windowing system also makes use of it.

Graphical kernel system - An international standard which models the screen as a collection of connected segments, each of which is a macro of elementary graphics commands.

Programmer’s hierarchical interface to graphics - Another international standard, based on GKS but with an extension to model the screen as editable segments.

Postscript - A computer language that was created by Adobe Corporation that represents the screen as a collection of pathways. These paths serve as endlessly thin limits or stencils, and they may be filled in with a variety of colors or textured patterns and pictures.

ARCHITECTURE OF WINDOWING SYSTEMS

In the mid-1980s, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) developed the industry-standard X Window System (Release 11), a window system based on the client–server architecture. Figure 8.3 illustrates X's software architecture. As previously stated, X (or X11) is based on a pixel-based imaging model and assumes a pointing mechanism is provided. The fact that X is built on a network protocol that clearly defines server–client communication distinguishes it from other window systems and explains why it has been adopted as a standard. The X Protocol can be implemented on several computers and operating systems, hence increasing the device independence of X.
